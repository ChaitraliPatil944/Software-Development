# Introduction

Unlike the Waterfall Model, Agile does not develop the entire product in one long sequence.

Instead, Agile develops software through **short, repeatable iterations**.

Each iteration delivers working software.

```text
Idea
  ↓
Small Release
  ↓
Feedback
  ↓
Improve
  ↓
Next Release
```

---

# What is the Agile Lifecycle?

The **Agile Lifecycle** is the continuous process that Agile teams follow to plan, develop, test, review, release, and improve software through short iterations (usually called **Sprints**).

The lifecycle repeats until the product goals are achieved.

# Step 1 — Product Vision

Everything begins with a business idea.

Examples:

- Food Delivery App
- Banking Platform
- Hospital Management System

The vision answers:

- Why are we building this?
- Who will use it?
- What problem are we solving?

Output:

- Product Vision
- Business Goals

---

# Step 2 — Product Backlog

The Product Backlog is a prioritized list of everything the product may need.

Example:

```text
Product Backlog

✓ User Login
✓ Search Products
✓ Shopping Cart
✓ Payments
✓ Notifications
```

The backlog changes throughout the project.

---

# Step 3 — Sprint Planning

The team selects backlog items for the next sprint.

Questions answered:

- What will we build?
- How will we build it?
- Who will work on each task?

Output:

- Sprint Goal
- Sprint Backlog

---

# Step 4 — Development

Developers implement the selected features.

Typical activities:

- Coding
- Unit Testing
- Code Reviews
- Integration

---

# Step 5 — Testing

QA verifies the sprint work.

Testing may include:

- Functional Testing
- Integration Testing
- Regression Testing
- Smoke Testing

Testing occurs continuously, not only at the end of the project.

---

# Step 6 — Sprint Review

The completed work is demonstrated to stakeholders.

```text
Agile Team
     |
Working Software
     |
Customer Feedback
```

Feedback may create new backlog items.

---

# Step 7 — Sprint Retrospective

The team reflects on the sprint.

Questions discussed:

- What went well?
- What went wrong?
- What should improve next sprint?

Purpose:

Continuous improvement.

---

# Step 8 — Release

If the increment is ready, it is released to users.

Not every sprint must end with a public release, but every sprint should produce a potentially releasable product increment.

---

# Step 9 — Customer Feedback

Users provide feedback after using the software.

Examples:

- Missing features
- Better UI suggestions
- Performance issues

Feedback updates the Product Backlog.

# Characteristics

- Short iterations
- Continuous planning
- Frequent testing
- Customer collaboration
- Continuous improvement
- Incremental delivery

---

# Agile Lifecycle vs Waterfall

| Waterfall | Agile |
|-----------|--------|
| One lifecycle | Repeating lifecycle |
| One release | Multiple releases |
| Feedback at end | Feedback every sprint |
| Fixed planning | Continuous planning |

---

# Best Practices

- Keep sprints short.
- Prioritize the backlog regularly.
- Deliver working software every sprint.
- Review progress with stakeholders.
- Improve after every retrospective.

---

# Common Mistakes

❌ Treating Agile like mini-Waterfall.

✔ Develop, test, and review continuously.

❌ Ignoring customer feedback.

✔ Update the backlog based on feedback.

❌ Skipping retrospectives.

✔ Improve the team's process every sprint.

# Summary

The Agile Lifecycle is a continuous cycle of planning, development, testing, review, release, and improvement. Instead of waiting until the end of a project, Agile teams deliver valuable software in small increments, gather feedback frequently, and refine the product over multiple sprints. This iterative approach enables organizations to respond quickly to changing business needs while maintaining high software quality.
