# Introduction

Most SDLC models rely on:

- Planning
- Documentation
- Design
- Defined phases

The **Big Bang Model** takes a very different approach.

Development starts with minimal planning, and the product evolves through experimentation.

```text
Idea
  |
Start Coding
  |
Experiment
  |
Working Product (if successful)
```

---

# What is the Big Bang Model?

The **Big Bang Model** is an SDLC model in which there is little formal planning, minimal documentation, and software is developed based primarily on available resources, experimentation, and evolving ideas.

The final outcome is uncertain.

---

# Why Was the Big Bang Model Used?

It emerged naturally in situations where:

- Requirements were unclear
- Research was the main goal
- Time spent on planning was not justified
- Teams wanted to validate an idea quickly

---

# Core Idea

```text
Idea
   ↓
Experiment
   ↓
Develop
   ↓
Test
   ↓
Result
```

Planning is minimal.

---

# Workflow

```text
Concept
    |
Basic Requirements
    |
Coding
    |
Testing
    |
Customer Feedback
    |
Modify
```

Unlike structured SDLC models, phases may overlap or occur in any order.

---

# Characteristics

- Minimal documentation
- Minimal planning
- Rapid experimentation
- Flexible direction
- High uncertainty
- Informal process

---

# Industry Example

## AI Research Prototype

Objective:

Test whether a new AI algorithm improves image classification.

Activities:

- Collect sample data
- Build a quick model
- Evaluate results
- Modify algorithm
- Repeat

If results are promising, the project may later move to a structured SDLC model.

---

# Advantages

- Very fast to start
- Encourages innovation
- Low planning overhead
- Suitable for experiments
- Useful for proof-of-concept work

---

# Limitations

- High project risk
- Difficult cost estimation
- Weak documentation
- Difficult maintenance
- Unpredictable schedule
- Not suitable for large teams

---

# Best Use Cases

Suitable for:

- Research projects
- Academic experiments
- Proof of Concept (PoC)
- Hackathons
- Small personal projects

Avoid for:

- Banking
- Healthcare
- Government systems
- Enterprise software
- Safety-critical systems

---

# Big Bang vs Waterfall

| Waterfall | Big Bang |
|-----------|----------|
| Structured | Unstructured |
| Heavy planning | Minimal planning |
| Predictable | Unpredictable |
| Strong documentation | Little documentation |

---

# Big Bang vs Prototype

| Big Bang | Prototype |
|----------|-----------|
| Focus on experimentation | Focus on understanding requirements |
| Informal | Structured feedback cycle |
| No defined prototype process | Prototype is central |

# Risks

```text
Unclear Requirements

Poor Estimation

Unexpected Changes
        ↓
Budget Overruns
```

---

# Common Mistakes

❌ Using the Big Bang Model for enterprise software.

✔ Use structured models for business-critical systems.

❌ Ignoring documentation completely.

✔ Record key technical decisions even in experiments.

❌ Expecting predictable delivery dates.

✔ Accept uncertainty as part of the model.

# Summary

The Big Bang Model emphasizes experimentation over structured planning. It is useful for research, proof-of-concept development, and very small projects where requirements are uncertain and rapid exploration is more valuable than formal process. Because it offers little control over cost, schedule, and quality, it is generally unsuitable for large or mission-critical software systems.
