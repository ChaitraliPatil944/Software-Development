# Introduction

Software is built for people, organizations, and businesses.

Every person or group affected by a software system is a stakeholder.

```text
Software Project

        ↓

Stakeholders

        ↓

Requirements

        ↓

Software Solution
```

Understanding stakeholders helps teams build the right product.

---

# What is a Stakeholder?

A **stakeholder** is any person, group, or organization that can influence or is affected by a software project.

Examples:

- Customers
- Users
- Managers
- Developers
- Business owners
- Government authorities

---

# Why Do We Need Stakeholder Analysis?

Without understanding stakeholders:

```text
Wrong Stakeholder Expectations

        ↓

Wrong Requirements

        ↓

Product Issues

        ↓

Project Failure
```

Stakeholder Analysis helps:

- Identify important people
- Understand expectations
- Manage conflicts
- Prioritize requirements
- Improve communication

---

# Stakeholder Identification

The first step is finding all stakeholders.

Questions:

- Who uses the system?
- Who pays for the system?
- Who manages the system?
- Who is affected by system decisions?

---

# Types of Stakeholders

## 1. Primary Stakeholders

Directly interact with the system.

Examples:

- Customers
- End Users
- Operators

---

## 2. Secondary Stakeholders

Indirectly affected.

Examples:

- Managers
- Business partners
- Support teams

---

## 3. External Stakeholders

Outside the organization.

Examples:

- Government bodies
- Regulatory authorities
- Vendors

---

# Stakeholder Classification

Stakeholders are classified based on:

- Power
- Interest
- Influence
- Impact

---

# Power Interest Matrix

A common stakeholder analysis technique.

```
              HIGH POWER

                  |
                  |
 Manage Closely   |   Keep Satisfied
                  |
------------------|------------------
                  |
 Monitor          |   Keep Informed
                  |
              LOW POWER


        LOW INTEREST        HIGH INTEREST
```

---

# 1. Manage Closely

High Power + High Interest

Example:

Product Owner

Strategy:

- Frequent communication
- Active involvement

---

# 2. Keep Satisfied

High Power + Low Interest

Example:

Senior Management

Strategy:

- Provide important updates
- Avoid unnecessary details

---

# 3. Keep Informed

Low Power + High Interest

Example:

End Users

Strategy:

- Regular communication
- Collect feedback

---

# 4. Monitor

Low Power + Low Interest

Example:

Indirect users

Strategy:

- Minimal communication

# Managing Conflicting Stakeholders

Example:

Customer:

"Make login faster."

Security Team:

"Add more authentication steps."

Conflict:

Speed vs Security

Solution:

Analyze business needs and find balance.

## Management

# Common Mistakes

## Mistake 1

Ignoring less visible stakeholders.

Solution:

Identify everyone affected.

---

## Mistake 2

Communicating the same way with everyone.

Solution:

Customize communication.

---

## Mistake 3

Not managing conflicts.

Solution:

Prioritize based on business value.

# Summary

Stakeholder Analysis helps software teams understand the people and organizations involved in a project. By identifying stakeholders, analyzing their power and interest, and managing communication effectively, teams reduce conflicts and ensure that software delivers real business and user value.
